Phylogeny and classification

Casparya was formerly treated as a separate section from Semibegoniella, even when both section share the same kind of fruit. Support for separation of Semibegoniella was based on the apophyllous perianth, simple placenta (vs. bifid). However there were difficulties with the placement of some species that have intermediate characters, as the case of Begonia killipiana, with red flowers but bifid placenta, or Begonia urticae that can have both types of placenta.  As show by \citet{Jara_Mu_oz_2019}, there are four main clades in Casparya that can be recognized according to morphological characters. Habitat and  distribution

Casparya ranges from the Province of Limón in Costa Rica to the state of Junín in Perú. In Central America there is only the endemic species, \textit{Begonia wilburii} Burt-Utley $\&$ Utley, and the wide distributed \textit{Begonia urticae} L.f. The pluvial region in the west of Colombia to north-west Ecuador, include mainly species formerly included in the section \textit{Semibegoniella}. The north of the Colombian eastern Cordillera and cordillera de Mérida, include are the richer area, with the number of species decreasing to the south. In Peru there is one endemic species Begonia hirta (Klotzsch) L.B. Sm. & B.G. Schub., and B. urticae.
Casparya species occurs primarily in well conserved forest from 500 to 3.500 m (Figure 2), more than two-thirds of the species have mean elevations between 2.000 and 3.000 m. The species that grow at lower elevations are Begonia irmscheri L.B.Sm. & B.G.Schub., Begonia libera (L.B.Sm. & B.G.Schub.) L.B.Sm. & B.G.Schub. and Begonia valvata L.B.Sm. & B.G.Schub., all of these growing in very humid forest. Some species have broad elevation ranges, being B. urticae the sidest, 1150–3340 m (2190 m), some species have narrow ranges as B. ursina and B. irmscheri, however most of the species have ranges narrower than 500 m, and there are not apparent relation between the median elevation and the size of the elevation range (Figure 3).
