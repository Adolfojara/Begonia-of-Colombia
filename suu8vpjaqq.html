Herb or subffrutex (erect), 0.3–1 m. Branches glabrous (as well as almost the whole plant), surface tuberculate. Stipules early deciduous, 14.4–27.3 mm long, × 6.1–11.4 mm, elliptic, membranaceous, hialine, acute or obtuse (rarely), margin entire. Petiole 5.4–30.6 mm long. Foliar lamina mostly straight; 7.3–17.2 cm; × 3.6–8.5 cm; ovate; slightly asymmetryc; base rounded; apex acuminate; margin doubly serrate; above glabrous, or with some hairs between each pair of secondary veins; short-villose on the veins; beneath glabrous; 6–8 veins pairs. Infloresence bisexual and male; male flowers distal and female flowers basal; protandrous; cincinum; 0 times divided; peduncle 26.7–69.3 mm long; bracts deciduous; oblong to eliptic; 7–14.3 mm long.; × 2.6–8.4 mm; apex acute; margin entire. Male inflorescence as the bisexual inflorescence. Staminate flowers: pedicels 8.8–12.3 mm long.; perianth with 4 tepals; partially connate (about 1/4 of the total lengh, the internaln ones free); red; glabrous; the external ones size 8.2–13.1 × 2.9–14.8 mm; suborvicular; rounded to bilobulate; the internal ones ca. 2.8 × 4.4 mm; flavelate; apex rounded, or truncate; stamens 6; free; filaments ca. 0.3 mm long.; anthers 1.8 × 0.5; oblong-obovate; conective few produced, obtuse. Pistillate Flowers: Pedicel 0.2–1 mm; tepals 5 tepals; partially fused (on the base); 13.5–14.0 × 12.9–13.5 mm; obovate; apex rounded; margin entire; the inner tepal smaller, ca. 14.4 × 7.8 mm,; oblong to obovate; styles multifid; ca. 2.9 mm long.; Stygma linear. Fruit 20.2–23.5 × 25.9–42.4 mm; column 8.4–22.6 mm; horns slightly onward; tip apiculate.

Habitat and distribution

This species grows in sympatry with B. suaviola, even sharing the same microhabitat. However, B. suaviola has not been reported from the Cerro Torrá (Valle del Cauca), and B. silverstonii has not been reported as northern as Alto Galápago (on the border between Chocó and Valle del Cauca departments) (Fig. 4).

Taxonomic notes

Because of the number of stamens (6), the double tepal series in the staminate flowers, and the absence of a small inner tepal series in the pistillate ones, this species resembles Begonia libera. It differs from that species, however, by the characters cited in the diagnosis. There are few collections of B. libera and all come from ca. 140 km to the south of the type locality of B. silverstonii, on the same Western Cordillera, at a lower elevation (1000–1400 m) than B. silverstonii (1800–2500).
