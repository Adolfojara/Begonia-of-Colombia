Diagnosis

Begonia mamapachensis is morphologically similar to Begonia cornuta, Begonia guanentana, and Begonia galeanoi,  but can be distinguished from them by having leaves lanceolate (vs. ovate or oblong), oblong (vs. ovate), and inflorescence a monochasium with few flowers (vs. dichasial with many flowers).

Etymology

Refers to the name of the mountain to which this species is endemic, the Cerro Mamapacha in the Colombian department of Boyacá, shared by the municipalities of Garagoa and Chinavita.  

Habitat and distribution

Begonia mamapachensis is endemic to the mountains known as Cerro Mamapacha, in a range between 2100 and 2500 m. Plants of the species has been found growing in the understory of the cloud forest, usually along tracks and stream borders.

Proposed IUCN conservation status

Because the extent of occurrence (43000 km2) the species is critically endangered (CR), and according to the area of occupancy (28000 km2) it is endangered (EN). The region where the species grows has a moderate intervention of agriculture and pasture, and is not part of any national or regional protected area, however, there are private reserves in the zone of Cerro Mamapacha. The plant is cultivated now in the special collection for conservation (CEPAC) at the Bogotá Botanic Garden.

Habitat and distribution

This species have a partially overlapping distribution with B. suaviola, even sharing the same microhabitat and flowering simultaneously. B. suaviola has not been reported from the Cerro Torrá (Valle del Cauca), and B. silverstonei has not been reported as northern as Alto Galápago (on the border between Chocó and Valle del Cauca departments)  

Notes

Because of its 6 stamens, the two series of tepals in staminate flowers, and the absence of a small inner tepal series in the pistillate flowers (versus inner tepals series about equaling the stiles like in B. kalbreyeri), this species resembles Begonia libera. It differs from that species, however, by the characters cited in the diagnosis. There are few collections of B. libera and all come from ca. 140 km to the south of the type locality of B. silverstonii, on the same Western Cordillera, at a lower elevation (1000–1400 m) than B. silverstonii (2000–2500).
