Etymology

This species honors the colombian botanist Gloria Galeano (1958-2016), who was a specialist in taxonomy and systematics of plants, mainly of the families Arecaceae and Cyclantaceae, and also studied the flora of the Choco region. In his thirty years in the Instituto de Ciencias Naturales Dr. Galeano collected hundreds of specimens, included ones of the first collections of the species here described.

Distribution adn habitat

Distribution and habitat

 Begonia guanentanta is endemic to the cloud forest  of the Colombian Eastern Cordillera, with a narrow distribution restricted to the locality of Virolín and contiguous vicinities, between 1700 and 2500 m. The forest of this region is dominated by Quercus humboldtii (Fagaceae), accompanied by highly diverse families such as Rubiaceae, Melastomataceae and Lauraceae in genera such as Miconia, Anthurium and Psychotria  \cite{Reina_2004}. In the region there has been recorded several biogeographicaly interesting species, such as the monotypic Colombobalanus excelsa (Lozano, Hern. Cam. & Henao, Jesús Eugenio) Nixon & Crepet, only known from three other localities in Colombia \cite{Aguirre_Acosta_2018}, as well as the endemic species: Arthrostylidium virolinense Londoño & L.G. Clark, Eugenia fernandez-alonsoi Parra-Os., and Magnolia virolinensis (Lozano) Govaerts.

Conservation status

Due to the extent of occurrence (43000 km2) the species is critically endangered (CR), and according to the area of occupancy (28000 km2) the species is endangered (EN). The region where the species grows has a moderate intervention of agriculture and pasture, however the region includes a protected area, the Santuario de Flora y Fauna de Iguaque, which recently was extended to the Cuchilla del Fara.

Habitat and distribution 

Grows on central part of the Cordillera Central on the western side. According to MacDougal et al. 3848, this species grows is "common in wet gorges, specially on rocks and humus/vertical slopes near stream". It has also seen growing in forest border.  

Taxonomic notes

According to Smith (1950), this variety differ from the typical variety in being completely glabrous, however the type specimien is clearely not glabrous, having hairs mainly on veins of the leaves, stems and inflorescences. I maintain this variety because the consistent precence of a type of hairs overside, symilar to the hairs in Begonia logirostris but less abundant, one or two big hairs on each space between secundaty veins. Differer from Begonia suaviola on the absence of tuberculate pubescence on the leaves.
